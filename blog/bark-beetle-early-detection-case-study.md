# Case Study: How Satellite Data Detected Bark Beetle Damage and Saved €186,000

In August 2025, a private forest owner in Central Finland logged into ForestData to review the latest satellite data for a 310-hectare spruce-dominated estate near Jyväskylä. The stand was scheduled for final harvest in 2027, when the timber would reach peak financial maturity according to the growth model. What the data revealed changed that plan entirely — and saved an estimated €186,000 in timber value.

## The Forest

The Keuruu estate had been in the family for three generations. At 65 years old, the Norway spruce (*Picea abies*) stand was approaching optimal harvest age. The most recent field inventory, conducted in 2023, showed healthy growth and an estimated standing volume of 245 m³/ha.

**Stand profile at the time of the 2023 inventory:**

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| Total area | 310 ha |
| Dominant species | Norway spruce (78%), Scots pine (15%), Birch (7%) |
| Mean stand age | 65 years |
| Standing volume | 245 m³/ha |
| Estimated timber value | €4.2M (at 2023 stumpage prices) |
| Planned harvest | Q2 2027 |

Based on the 2023 numbers and a 3% discount rate, the owner's forest management plan recommended waiting until 2027 for the final cut. The NPV analysis showed roughly €320,000 in additional value from two more years of growth.

## The Warning Signs

When the owner checked ForestData in August 2025, two indices immediately stood out.

### NDMI Drop

The **Normalized Difference Moisture Index (NDMI)** tracks canopy water content. Healthy spruce stands in this region typically show summer NDMI values between 0.35 and 0.50. ForestData's vegetation statistics dashboard showed that the stand's median NDMI had dropped from 0.42 in June to 0.29 in August — a 31% decline in just two months.

More telling was the spatial pattern. The NDMI percentile distribution revealed that the lowest 25th percentile had collapsed to 0.14, indicating that a significant portion of the stand was severely moisture-stressed. The moisture imagery overlay on the map showed the affected area concentrated in the southeastern section, roughly 85 hectares.

### NDVI Anomaly

The NDVI time series, which had shown steady improvement for nine consecutive years, flatlined in July and began declining. The 10-year trend line projected continued growth, but actual values diverged sharply downward. ForestData flagged this as an anomalous deviation from the expected seasonal pattern.

### NDRE Confirmation

The **Normalized Difference Red Edge (NDRE)** index, which is sensitive to chlorophyll content in dense canopies where NDVI can saturate, confirmed the stress signal. NDRE values in the affected area were 0.18 — well below the stand average of 0.31 — indicating that the trees were losing photosynthetic capacity before any visible crown discoloration.

## The Diagnosis

The combination of rapidly declining moisture content (NDMI), stalled vegetation vigor (NDVI), and reduced chlorophyll activity (NDRE) pointed clearly to one cause: **European spruce bark beetle** (*Ips typographus*).

The summer of 2025 had been unusually warm and dry in Central Finland, creating ideal conditions for bark beetle population explosions. Drought-stressed spruce trees produce less resin, weakening their primary defense mechanism against boring insects.

A targeted field visit confirmed what the satellite data had already revealed. The southeastern section showed:

- Fresh boring dust (brown frass) at the base of trunks
- Entry holes in the bark of mature spruce
- Blue-stain fungus (*Endoconidiophora polonica*) beginning to spread in sapwood
- Early crown yellowing not yet visible from a distance, but obvious up close

The infestation was in its second generation of the season, meaning the beetle population was expanding exponentially.

## The Decision

The owner faced a stark choice:

**Option A: Wait until 2027 as planned.**
The bark beetle population would continue spreading through the winter-weakened trees. Based on Finnish Forest Centre data, untreated *Ips typographus* infestations in similar stands have historically degraded 40-60% of the timber volume within 12-18 months. Sawlog-quality wood downgrades to pulpwood or worse as blue-stain fungus penetrates the sapwood.

**Option B: Initiate immediate salvage harvest.**
Harvest the affected southeastern section immediately and accelerate the schedule for the remaining stand to winter 2025-2026, before the beetles could spread further.

The financial comparison was decisive:

| Scenario | Sawlog volume | Pulpwood volume | Estimated value |
|----------|--------------|-----------------|-----------------|
| Wait until 2027 (with beetle spread) | ~98,000 m³ | ~48,000 m³ | €3.05M |
| Immediate harvest (Aug-Dec 2025) | ~152,000 m³ | ~24,000 m³ | €4.01M |
| Original 2027 plan (no beetles) | ~168,000 m³ | ~18,000 m³ | €4.52M |

The beetle damage had already cost roughly €510,000 in potential value compared to the original plan. But acting immediately would preserve €4.01M versus the €3.05M projected under continued infestation — a difference of €960,000. Compared to the alternative of waiting and losing sawlog quality across the stand, the early detection saved approximately **€186,000** in net terms after accounting for the lost growth premium and accelerated harvesting costs.

## The Harvest

The owner contracted a local harvesting company within two weeks of the satellite detection. The operation proceeded in two phases:

**Phase 1 (September 2025):** Salvage harvest of the 85-hectare southeastern section. Infested trees were prioritized. Logs were transported directly to the mill to minimize blue-stain spread. Despite the damage, 71% of the volume from this section graded as sawlog quality — largely because the infestation was caught early, before the fungus had penetrated deep into the sapwood.

**Phase 2 (November-December 2025):** Accelerated harvest of the remaining 225 hectares. With the beetle source removed and cold weather halting beetle activity, this timber was harvested in excellent condition. Sawlog recovery rate was 87%, consistent with a healthy mature stand.

## Lessons Learned

### 1. Multi-Index Monitoring Catches What the Eye Misses

NDVI alone would have raised a flag eventually, but the NDMI moisture signal was the earliest and clearest indicator. Bark beetle damage affects water transport in the sapwood before it visibly affects the crown. By the time a forester notices yellowing needles from the forest road, the beetles have often already moved on to adjacent trees.

### 2. Percentile Distributions Matter More Than Averages

The stand-level median NDMI dropped from 0.42 to 0.29, which is concerning but not alarming in isolation — a dry summer could explain it. What made the signal unambiguous was the **percentile distribution**: the 25th percentile collapsed to 0.14 while the 75th percentile held at 0.38, revealing that the damage was concentrated rather than uniform. ForestData's vegetation statistics dashboard made this spatial pattern immediately visible.

### 3. The Cost of Delayed Detection Compounds Exponentially

Bark beetle populations can double every 6-8 weeks during warm conditions. Each generation expands the infestation front. A two-month delay in detection could have meant the difference between losing 85 hectares and losing the entire 310-hectare stand. The satellite's 5-day revisit cycle provides the temporal resolution needed to catch fast-moving threats.

### 4. Early Harvest Is Not Failure

The Keuruu estate lost two years of planned growth. But the alternative — watching beetle-killed timber degrade from sawlog to pulpwood quality — would have cost far more. The data gave the owner confidence to make a difficult decision quickly, backed by quantifiable evidence rather than guesswork.
